## 4. Methodology Overview

The Corruptibility Index™ evaluates eleven independent domains of behavior and structure. Each domain is assessed using publicly observable data and standardized into a 0–100 scale.

Metrics are evaluated independently and then combined into an aggregate risk score. The CI assesses patterns and structures, not intent or wrongdoing.

## 6. Endnotes

**1. Donor Metrics Justification**  
Research in political finance demonstrates that donor concentration, cluster coordination, and repeat-entity ecosystems are strong indicators of influence centralization. High-density donation networks increase structural risk due to potential gatekeeping and policy leverage. Standards referenced include FEC guidance, academic studies on political donor networks, and transparency benchmarks for campaign ecosystems.

**2. Wealth Anomaly Justification**  
Unusual changes in reported wealth or income, when not paired with corresponding disclosures, are recognized indicators of structural financial irregularity. These patterns align with governance frameworks from international organizations that monitor asset-movement anomalies as a signal of potential opacity.

**3. Real Estate Patterning Justification**  
Real estate is a common vehicle for obscured financial activity. Multi-layered LLC ownership, below-market transfers, and rapid re-transfer patterns are widely documented markers of opacity risk. International property-transparency initiatives and governance research consistently classify such indicators as relevant structural-risk factors.

**4. Lobbyist Entanglement Justification**  
Overlap between lobbying ecosystems and financial support networks is a recognized structural-risk factor, as documented in lobbying-transparency literature. Network density between lobbyists, donors, and policymakers is associated with increased influence pressure.

**5. Transactional Appointments Justification**  
Appointment patterns have long been analyzed by governance researchers as signals of patronage ecosystems. Appointing individuals with direct ties to donors or financially aligned organizations can elevate structural risk by increasing the likelihood of reciprocal arrangements.

**6. Policy Misalignment Risk Justification**  
Inconsistencies between stated positions and policy actions can indicate external pressures or misaligned incentives. Comparative political-behavior research treats policy drift as a meaningful risk signal when correlated with contributory or financial ecosystems.

**7. Social Ecosystem Alignment Justification**  
Associational patterns and social-network structures influence exposure to risk environments. Low-risk social networks correlate with reduced probability of influence from opaque or high-risk actors.

**8. Personal Conduct Anomalies Justification**  
Patterns in personal conduct are relevant to assessing decision-making environments. Although isolated incidents may not be significant, recurring or escalating patterns are associated with increased structural risk.

**9. Legal Irregularities Justification**  
Administrative and legal interactions provide insight into transparency and compliance habits. Recurring issues or escalating irregularities are widely recognized as governance red flags.

**10. Casino License Heuristic Justification**  
Industries with high regulatory and financial transparency requirements have developed heuristic patterns for identifying financial opacity. Multi-layered ownership chains, circular transactions, and unclear beneficial ownership are commonly monitored attributes.

**11. Investigative Pressure Index Justification**  
Sustained scrutiny by media, watchdogs, and public records requesters reflects external identification of potential vulnerabilities. High investigative activity correlates with elevated structural-risk environments due to independent detection of anomalies.
